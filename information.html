WHY

would I go vegan

FOR THE PLANET


The production of meat and other animal derived products places a heavy burden on the environment.
The vast amount of grain feed required for meat production is a significant contributor to deforestation, habitat loss and species extinction. In Brazil alone, the equivalent of 5.6 million acres of land is used to grow soya beans for animals in Europe. This land contributes to developing world malnutrition by driving impoverished populations to grow cash crops for animal feed, rather than food for themselves.
On the other hand, considerably lower quantities of crops and water are required to sustain populations on a vegan diet, making the switch to veganism one of the easiest, most enjoyable and most effective ways to reduce our impact on the environment.
Did you know that animal agriculture is accountable for more greenhouse gases than all the world's transportation systems combined?

FOR YOUR HEALTH


Well-planned vegan diets follow the major healthy eating guidelines, and contain all the nutrients that our bodies need. Both the British Dietetic Association and the American Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics recognise that they are suitable for every age and stage of life.

Some of the several potential health benefits include:


Lower risk of heart disease: A vegan diet can lower cholesterol and blood pressure, which are risk factors for heart disease.


Lower risk of some types of cancer: A vegan diet has been linked to a lower risk of certain types of cancer, such as colorectal and breast cancer.


Lower risk of type 2 diabetes: A vegan diet may help lower the risk of type 2 diabetes by improving insulin sensitivity and reducing body fat.


Weight management: A vegan diet can help with weight management because it is typically low in calories and high in fiber.


Improved gut health: A vegan diet can improve gut health by increasing the consumption of fiber and reducing the consumption of unhealthy fats.

FOR THE ANIMALS


Ethical vegans have made the link that there is no difference between a dog and a pig or a cat and a fish. They are all sentient beings and creatures that want to live. Furthermore, ethical vegans find factory farming cruel and nothing like the marketing adverts showing happy cows grazing in lush green fields. Creatures bred for our plates live short and miserable lives, therefore the only way ethical vegans can see to protect them is by abstaining from all animal products.


Animals are sentient creatures that have a right to live. Ethical vegans believe that killing to eat is wrong. Why do we protect dogs from being hit by punishing perpetrators yet encourage pig farmers due to the demand for bacon? How can one page in a magazine tell readers to adopt a cat from a shelter, while the other side advertises fresh Atlantic salmon?


Ethical Vegans are those who have chosen a vegan lifestyle, as they are firmly against animal-cruelty. This type of vegans don't want their life to involve exploiting animals in anyway. About a third of people who choose to go vegan, do it for the animals.

HOW

would I do veganism

START SLOW


Transitioning to a vegan lifestyle can seem daunting, but there are many resources and support systems available to help. Here are some steps to help with the transition:


Educate yourself: Read about the benefits of a vegan diet and the ethical and environmental issues surrounding animal agriculture.


Start slow: Gradually reduce the amount of animal products in your diet and experiment with new plant-based foods.


Find alternatives: Discover new plant-based substitutes for your favorite animal products, such as vegan meat and dairy alternatives.


Get creative in the kitchen: Try new recipes and experiment with different spices and ingredients to make your meals exciting and delicious.


Connect with others: Join a local vegan community or online group to connect with others who are on the same journey and to share advice and recipes.


Remember that it might be a journey that takes time. Be patient and kind to yourself, and seek help or support when needed.


WHAT

would I eat as vegan

DIVERSIFY


Developed based upon the best available evidence, the 'Daily Dozen' is an excelent guideline developed by Dr. Michael Greger M.D. (founder of NutritionFacts.org), containing the healthiest foods and respective dozage that one should strive to include every day, even if not on strict plant-based diets.


That food list is the following:


Beans Servings: 3 per day (e.g. ½ c. cooked beans, ¼ c. hummus).


Berries Servings: 1 per day (e.g. ½ c. fresh or frozen, ¼ c. dried).


Other Fruits Servings: 3 per day (e.g. 1 medium fruit, ¼ c. dried fruit).


Cruciferous Vegetables Servings: 1 per day (e.g. ½ c. chopped, 1 tbsp horseradish).


Greens Servings: 2 per day (e.g. 1 c. raw, ½ c. cooked).


Other Vegetables Servings: 2 per day (e.g. ½ c. nonleafy vegetables).


Flaxseed Servings: 1 per day (e.g. 1 tbsp ground).


Nuts and Seeds Servings: 1 per day (e.g. ¼ c. nuts, 2 tbsp nut butter).


Herbs and Spices Servings: 1 per day (e.g. ¼ tsp turmeric).


Whole Grains Servings: 3 per day (e.g. ½ c. hot cereal, 1 slice of bread).


Beverages Servings: 60 oz per day (e.g. Water, green tea, hibiscus tea).


Exercise Servings: Once per day (e.g. 90 min. moderate or 40 min. vigorous).


Suplements

Vitamin B12 - At least 2,000 mcg (µg) cyanocobalamin once each week (or at least 50 mcg daily), ideally as a chewable, sublingual, or liquid supplement taken on an empty stomach.

Vitamin D - Suplementation of is only needed for people who get inadequate sun exposure.
